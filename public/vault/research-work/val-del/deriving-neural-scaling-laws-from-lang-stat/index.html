Deriving Neural Scaling Laws from Lang Stat

January 1, 0001

1. Foundational Setup and Definitions

A. Autoregressive Test Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{AR}$)

In autoregressive language modeling, the network predicts the probability of the next token $X_{n+1}$ given a sequence of past conditioning tokens $X_{1:n}$. For a maximum context window length $T$, the global autoregressive loss is defined as the arithmetic mean of individual $n$-gram cross-entropy losses over all available horizons $n \in [1, T]$: $$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) = \frac{1}{T} \sum{n=1}^{T} \mathcal{L}n(P)$$ where the $n$-gram loss at a specific context length is given by: $$\mathcal{L}n(P) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\log \hat{p}(X{n+1}|X{1:n})\right]$$

B. Horizon Differential Loss ($\Delta_n$)

To isolate the exact performance gain achieved by expanding the conditioning window by a single historical token, we introduce the differential loss: $$\Delta_n(P) = \mathcal{L}n(P) - \mathcal{L}{n-1}(P)$$ This formulation allows us to rewrite the total autoregressive loss as a telescoping sum of differential adjustments anchored by the unigram loss $\mathcal{L}0$: $$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) = \mathcal{L}0 + \sum{n=1}^{T} \frac{T - (n - 1)}{T} \Delta_n(P) \approx H_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{T} \Delta_n(P)$$

2. Core Statistical Scaling Hypotheses

Hypothesis 1: Power-Law Decay of Conditional Entropy ($\gamma$)

The structural information limit of any $n$-gram loss is bounded below by the true conditional entropy of the data distribution, $H_n = \lim_{P \to \infty} \mathcal{L}n(P)$. As context length $n$ expands, the conditional entropy decreases toward its asymptotic value $H\infty$ according to a power law governed by the exponent $\gamma$: $$H_n - H_\infty \approx n^{-\gamma}$$ Taking the continuous derivative with respect to $n$ yields the marginal change in entropy introduced by the $n$-th historical token: $$H_n - H_{n-1} = \frac{dH_n}{dn} \approx -n^{-\gamma-1}$$

Hypothesis 2: Power-Law Decay of Token Correlations ($\beta$)

The statistical dependence between pairs of tokens separated by a temporal lag $n$ is captured by the $V \times V$ token-token covariance matrix $C(n)$. The maximum strength of this statistical signal—measured by the operator norm (top singular value) of the matrix—decays as a power law with the separation distance $n$, governed by the exponent $\beta$: $$|C(n)|_{op} \approx n^{-\beta}$$

3. The Data-Dependent Prediction Horizon ($n^*$)

A. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) Bounding

A model can only exploit structural associations at a distance $n$ if the true correlation signal stands out clearly against the empirical sampling noise generated by a finite dataset of size $P$. Standard statistical sampling variance introduces an empirical background noise floor scaled by the dataset size:

$$\text{Sampling Noise} \sim \mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{P}}\right)$$

For successful feature discovery and resolution, the true signal must cross this noise threshold. This sets up the fundamental tracking inequality:

$$|C(n)|_{op} > \frac{c}{\sqrt{P}} \implies n^{-\beta} > \frac{c}{\sqrt{P}}$$

B. Analytical Mapping of Thresholds

Squaring both sides and isolating $P$ yields the time-dependent data threshold $P_n^*$, which defines the minimum number of training tokens required to reliably resolve dependencies at a horizon $n$:

$$P_n^* \approx n^{2\beta}$$ Inverting this functional relationship allows us to define the data-dependent maximal prediction time horizon, $n^*(P)$. This represents the maximum context length a model can effectively exploit when constrained by a total training dataset size $P$:

$$n^*(P) \approx P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}$$

4. Bounding and Decomposing the Loss Profile

For context steps beyond the data-dependent threshold ($n > n^(P)$), the available sample size $P$ is insufficient to resolve token dependencies from random noise. Consequently, the model receives no statistical benefit from this extended context, causing the differential step loss to vanish: $$\Delta_n(P) \approx 0 \quad \text{for } n > n^(P)$$ This observation allows us to truncate the continuous summation range of our autoregressive loss expression at $n^(P)$: $$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) - H{\infty} \approx \sum_{n=1}^{n^(P)} \Delta_n(P) - H_\infty$$

We decouple this active sum into two distinct physical processes: the theoretical entropy limits of the unlocked context window, and the suboptimal optimization penalty ($\mathcal{E}_n$) representing the excess loss within that unlocked window:

$$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) - H{\infty} \approx \sum_{n=1}^{n^(P)} (H_n - H_{n-1}) + \sum_{n=1}^{n^(P)} \mathcal{E}_n(P)$$

Collapsing the telescoping entropy sum collapses the primary boundary term: $$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) - H{\infty} \approx \left(H_{n^(P)} - H_\infty\right) + \sum_{n=1}^{n^(P)} \mathcal{E}_n(P)$$

5. Integrating Scaling Components to Derive the Exponent

Term 1: Resolution of the Boundary Target

Substituting the data-dependent maximum context horizon $n^*(P) \approx P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}$ directly into the conditional entropy decay function ($n^{-\gamma}$) yields the loss component dictated purely by the expanding horizon limit: $$\text{Boundary Loss Component} \approx \left(P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}\right)^{-\gamma} = P^{-\frac{\gamma}{2\beta}}$$

Term 2: Evaluation of In-Horizon Suboptimality

For horizons safely within the resolvable limit ($P \gg P_n^*$), the excess optimization error $\mathcal{E}_n(P)$ decays relative to an asymptotic parameter learning rate $\delta$:

$$\mathcal{E}n(P) \approx -(H_n - H{n-1})\left(\frac{P_n^*}{P}\right)^\delta \approx n^{-\gamma-1}\left(\frac{n^{2\beta}}{P}\right)^\delta = \frac{1}{P^\delta} n^{2\beta\delta - \gamma - 1}$$

To evaluate the total performance drag within the horizon, we approximate the sum across the active context window using a definite integral:

$$\sum_{n=1}^{n^*(P)} \mathcal{E}n(P) \approx \frac{1}{P^\delta} \int{1}^{P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}} n^{2\beta\delta - \gamma - 1} , dn$$

Integrating the polynomial expression across the boundaries yields:

$$\int_{1}^{P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}} n^{2\beta\delta - \gamma - 1} , dn \approx \left[ \frac{n^{2\beta\delta - \gamma}}{2\beta\delta - \gamma} \right]_{1}^{P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}} \approx \frac{\left(P^{\frac{1}{2\beta}}\right)^{2\beta\delta - \gamma}}{2\beta\delta - \gamma} = \frac{P^{\delta - \frac{\gamma}{2\beta}}}{2\beta\delta - \gamma}$$

Multiplying this result back by the external scaling factor ($1/P^\delta$) reveals how the internal excess loss scales globally with $P$:

$$\text{Total Optimization Penalty Component} \approx \frac{1}{P^\delta} \cdot P^{\delta - \frac{\gamma}{2\beta}} = P^{-\frac{\gamma}{2\beta}}$$

6. Analytical Conclusion & Universality Class

Combining both components produces the comprehensive scaling relation for the excess autoregressive test loss:

$$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) - H{\infty} \approx P^{-\frac{\gamma}{2\beta}} + P^{-\delta} \implies P^{-\min\left(\delta, , \frac{\gamma}{2\beta}\right)}$$

If we assume fast learning regime i.e $\delta>\frac{\gamma}{2\beta}$ , the slower decaying term becomes dominant and we get the scaling Law given below based purely on the 2 params.

In this regime, the optimization error decays faster than the boundary error, meaning the performance bottleneck is determined entirely by the data requirements needed to resolve long-range token relationships. The relation simplifies to the clean power-law scaling rule:

$$\mathcal{L}{AR}(P) - H{\infty} \propto P^{-\alpha_D} \quad \text{where} \quad \alpha_D = \frac{\gamma}{2\beta}$$
